Diffusion Predictor? A Falsification Study of\\[2pt]
Token-Adaptive Prediction on an Exact-Residual Testbed\par}
\vspace{10pt}
{\large Bhavith Chandra Challagundla\par}
\vspace{3pt}
{\small New York University \;\textbullet\; \href{https://Bhavith-Chandra.github.io}{Bhavith-Chandra.github.io}\par}
\vspace{13pt}
\begin{minipage}{0.88\textwidth}
\small
\textbf{Abstract.} Training-free diffusion accelerators such as the Token-Adaptive
Predictor (TAP) speed up sampling by skipping most of the network and rebuilding the skipped
outputs from cached features. For each token, TAP picks which cheap predictor to trust by
reading a first-layer \emph{probe} and scoring the predictors with a cosine distance. We
test three possible improvements that looked good in an early single-seed run: using an
$L_2$ distance instead of cosine, using online least-squares predictors instead of fixed
Taylor ones, and letting the probe choose a predictor for each token. We test them on a
Gaussian-mixture testbed where the exact denoiser output is known in closed form (Tweedie),
so we can measure how well the probe ranks predictors without a trained network. We run a
$12$-seed core study, a $21$-regime sweep, and a pre-registered $30$-test suite, all with
$95\%$ bootstrap confidence intervals. Most of the improvements do not hold. (i) The $L_2$
distance ranks predictors better than cosine in all $21$ regimes, but the gain is small
($\Delta$Spearman $+0.016$, CI $(+0.014,+0.019)$), not the roughly fourfold gain seen in the
early run, and it grows only as the task gets harder. (ii) Online predictors do not beat
fixed Taylor ones; they are worse in $19$ of $21$ regimes. (iii) Letting the probe choose
per token beats a strong fixed predictor only when the sampler skips very aggressively; at
moderate speedups the speedup itself, not the per-token choice, explains the gains. We then
show that the per-token choice is already the best possible: each token is chosen on its
own, every predictor costs the same, and there are no shared constraints, so simply taking
the best predictor for each token is optimal. This shifts the real question to \emph{proxy
fidelity}: an oracle that knows the true errors still does better than the probe, and the
probe's ranking improves steadily as it is given a richer signal (from $0.33$ to $1.0$). We
also explain why an $L_2$ distance follows the true $L_2$ error better than a cosine
distance, which ignores size.\par
\vspace{4pt}
\textit{TAP (arXiv:2603.03792) has no public code, so everything about TAP here comes from
our own faithful reimplementation. Our testbed uses a simple analytic denoiser and does not
have the deep trained layers that make a real first-layer probe more useful; we measure that
gap instead of hiding it. All code and numbers are released.}
\end{minipage}
\end{center}

\section{Introduction}
Feature-caching accelerators for diffusion transformers (DiTs)~\cite{peebles2023dit} skip
most of the network on most sampling steps and rebuild the missing output from cached
values~\cite{ma2024deepcache,selvaraju2024fora}. The Token-Adaptive Predictor (TAP) extends
this idea: it keeps a set of cheap predictors and, for each token, picks one by reading the
first-layer \emph{probe} (the modulated input) and choosing the predictor whose guessed
probe is closest to the real probe, using a cosine distance. An early single-seed run
suggested three ways to do better: use an $L_2$ distance instead of cosine, use online
least-squares predictors instead of fixed Taylor ones, and let the probe pick a predictor
for each token. Here we test these ideas across many seeds and many settings to see which
ones hold.

Most of the results are negative, and we think they are more useful than one more caching
trick. Two of the three ideas fail once we use more than one seed, and the third gives only
a small but repeatable gain. The study also points to the thing that really matters. Because
TAP chooses each token on its own, every predictor costs the same, and there are no shared
limits, simply picking the best predictor for each token is already the best possible choice
(\S\ref{sec:theory}); there is nothing to gain by choosing more cleverly. What actually
limits the method is how well the cheap probe matches the true, hidden output error, and our
testbed measures this directly.

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.15ex}
\item A Gaussian-mixture testbed (Fig.~\ref{fig:arch}) where the true denoiser output, and
therefore every predictor's true error, is known in closed form. This lets us measure how
well the probe ranks predictors without a trained network.
\item A proof that TAP's per-token choice is already the best possible, which moves the
problem from ``choose better'' to ``measure the probe better,'' along with an explanation of
why an $L_2$ distance beats cosine.
\item A full evaluation ($12$-seed core, $21$-regime sweep, and a pre-registered $30$-test
suite; Fig.~\ref{fig:flow}) that rules out the online-predictor and per-token ideas and
shows the $L_2$ gain is small but real.
\end{itemize}

\section{Findings at a glance}\label{sec:findings}
We state the results upfront. Each one uses $12$ seeds (core) or $5$ seeds per cell (sweep)
with $95\%$ bootstrap CIs, and is re-checked by the $30$-test suite.
\begin{enumerate}\setlength\itemsep{0.25ex}\leftskip-2pt
\item[\textbf{F1}] \textbf{The $L_2$ distance beats cosine in every setting, but the gain is
small.} It ranks predictors better than cosine in \textbf{$21/21$} settings ($\Delta$Spearman
$+0.016$, CI $(+0.014,+0.019)$; top-$1$ pick $+0.028$), not the roughly fourfold gain from
the early run. $L_1$ does as well as $L_2$, so what matters is using size, not cosine itself.
The gain grows as the task gets harder, from $+0.012$ at mild skipping to $+0.034$ at cache
stride $8$.
\item[\textbf{F2}] \textbf{Online least-squares predictors do not beat fixed Taylor ones.}
They are worse (Taylor wins in \textbf{$19/21$} settings, online in $0/21$), and the best
single fixed predictor is always a Taylor one. The gain from the early run does not repeat.
\item[\textbf{F3}] \textbf{Letting the probe pick per token helps only when the sampler
skips very aggressively.} It beats the best fixed predictor in just \textbf{$1/21$} settings
(cache stride $8$); in every milder setting a single fixed predictor does just as well, so
the gains from ``adaptive'' caching at moderate speedups come from the speedup, not the
per-token choice.
\item[\textbf{F4}] \textbf{There is no room to improve the choice itself.} Each token is
chosen on its own, every predictor costs the same, and there are no shared limits, so picking
the best predictor per token is already the best possible (\S\ref{sec:theory}). An oracle
that knows the true errors still beats the naive baseline (gap $+0.005$, up to $+0.14$ at
stride $8$), so what limits the method is \emph{proxy fidelity}, not the choice rule or the
predictor set.
\item[\textbf{F5}] \textbf{Proxy fidelity depends on how rich the probe is.} As the probe
goes from a simple hard readout to the exact output, its ranking gets steadily better
($0.33$, $0.68$, $0.73$, $1.0$). A trained DiT's learned first-layer probe sits much higher
on this curve (Spearman ${\approx}0.77$~\cite{challagundla2025probe}), so our simple probe
gives a lower bound and the $L_2$ gain we measure is on the safe side.
\item[\textbf{F6}] \textbf{Method note.} The pre-registered $30$-test suite gives
\textbf{$26$ passes, $0$ failures, and $4$ notes}. Gains that show up with one seed shrink
or flip with more seeds, so these methods should be judged on trained networks using paired
fidelity-to-base-model scores.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and related work}
\textbf{Feature caching.} DeepCache~\cite{ma2024deepcache} and FORA~\cite{selvaraju2024fora}
reuse features on fixed schedules; TeaCache~\cite{liu2024teacache} modulates reuse by a
timestep-embedding indicator; ToCa~\cite{zou2024toca} and DiCache~\cite{dicache2025} make
the decision token- or sample-adaptive; TaylorSeer~\cite{taylorseer2025} replaces
cache-and-reuse with Taylor \emph{prediction} of features. TAP generalises the predictor to
a per-token \emph{selected} member of a small family. \textbf{Diffusion samplers.} We use
the variance-preserving forward process of DDPM~\cite{ho2020ddpm} with deterministic
DDIM~\cite{song2021ddim} reverse steps; the exact posterior mean follows from
Tweedie's formula~\cite{efron2011tweedie} and the score view of
diffusion~\cite{song2019generative,song2021sde}. \textbf{A pitfall in evaluation.} These
methods are usually compared by output quality at the same compute. One recurring problem,
which we make explicit and which led to this study, is that comparisons made on simple or
untrained denoisers may not carry over to trained ones; a companion study shows that the
probe only becomes reliable through training~\cite{challagundla2025probe}. \textbf{Status of
TAP.} TAP (arXiv:2603.03792) has no released code as of writing. Everything we report about
TAP comes from our own faithful rebuild of its probe-then-select method, so we test the
method rather than a specific codebase.

\section{Positioning of Latent-TAP}\label{sec:positioning}
Training-free accelerators differ along two axes: how fine-grained the caching decision is
(a global schedule, a per-timestep choice, a per-sample choice, or a per-token choice), and
how much work the rebuild does (plain feature reuse, reuse with rescaling, Taylor
prediction, or picking from a set of predictors). Figure~\ref{fig:landscape} places the main
methods in this space, and Table~\ref{tab:positioning} lists what each one does along with
the model and speedup it reports. Latent-TAP sits in the finest and most capable corner: for
each token it picks from a set of predictors, using the first-layer probe to decide. TAP
shares this corner but has no public code; this study adds an open rebuild and, more
importantly, an exact testbed where we can check the assumptions behind this corner instead
of taking them on faith. Because Latent-TAP runs on a simple analytic denoiser, we report
its quality as a paired energy distance to the full sampler (Table~\ref{tab:core}) rather
than a model FID, so it does not appear on the speedup axis of Figure~\ref{fig:landscape}(b).

\section{The exact-residual testbed}\label{sec:testbed}
We cannot measure proxy fidelity directly on a trained network, because computing the true
skipped output would cancel the speedup we are trying to get. So we use a data distribution
for which the exact denoiser is known in closed form. Data are drawn from a $K$-component
isotropic Gaussian
mixture in $\mathbb R^d$; under the variance-preserving forward process $x_t=\sqrt{\bar
\alpha_t}\,x_0+\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon$ the posterior mean is exactly
\begin{equation}
O_t \equiv \mathbb E[x_0\mid x_t]=\sum_{k=1}^{K} r_k(x_t,t)\,\big[\mu_k+c_t\,(x_t-\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\mu_k)\big],
\end{equation}
with responsibilities $r_k\!\propto\! w_k\,\mathcal N\!\big(x_t;\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\mu_k,(\bar\alpha_t\sigma^2{+}1{-}\bar\alpha_t)I\big)$
and $c_t=\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\sigma^2/(\bar\alpha_t\sigma^2{+}1{-}\bar\alpha_t)$. This is the
expensive output. The cheap \emph{probe} $h_t$ is the hard-assignment version of the
denoiser, which keeps only the top component (the $\arg\max_k r_k$ term). It stands in for a
shallow first-layer readout that sees an approximate, not exact, decision. Tokens are
i.i.d.\ draws from the mixture, and DDIM steps use $O$ at anchor steps and a predicted
$\hat O$ at skipped steps (Fig.~\ref{fig:arch}).

\textbf{Predictors.} A family of $8$: fixed finite-difference Taylor extrapolators of order
$0$--$2$ at horizons $1$--$2$, plus online least-squares fits (degree $2$--$3$ over windows
of $5$--$6$ anchors). \textbf{Proxy-then-select.} Each predictor extrapolates the cached
outputs ($\hat O_i$) and cached probes ($\hat h_i$); the selector picks per token
$\hat\imath=\arg\min_i d_\mu(\hat h_i,h_t)$ for proxy $\mu\in\{\cos,L_2,L_1\}$ and applies
$\hat O_{\hat\imath}$. \textbf{Oracle} instead minimises the true error $\|\hat
O_i-O_t\|_2$. \textbf{Metrics.} (a) the rank correlation between proxy error and true error
across the predictor family, which tells us whether the proxy puts the predictors in the
right order; and (b) the energy distance~\cite{szekely2013energy} between the final samples
of the accelerated and full samplers, which tells us how close the two output distributions
are. We use $12$ seeds for the core study and $5$ per regime cell, with bootstrap $95\%$
confidence intervals throughout.

\section{Optimality of greedy per-token selection}\label{sec:theory}
Let $f_n(i)$ be the true error of predictor $i$ on token $n$ at a skipped step. TAP
minimises the total error $\sum_n f_n(\pi_n)$ over choices $\pi$. Three things hold: the
total splits across tokens (token $n$'s cost depends only on its own choice $\pi_n$), every
predictor costs the same (one cheap extrapolation, with no shared budget), and any token may
take any predictor. Hence
\begin{equation}
\min_{\pi}\ \sum_{n} f_n(\pi_n)\;=\;\sum_n \min_i f_n(i),
\end{equation}
so picking the best predictor for each token on its own already gives the lowest total
error. A regret bound or knapsack version of the joint choice therefore solves a problem
that has no gap to close, which was the mistake in the early framing. The real difficulty is
not the choice but the measurement: we never see the true errors $f_n(i)$. Instead we see a
proxy $g_n(i)=d_\mu(\hat h_i,h)$ and use $\hat\pi_n=\arg\min_i g_n(i)$. The extra error
$\mathbb E[f_n(\hat\pi_n)-\min_i f_n(i)]$ depends only on how well the proxy order $g$
matches the true order $f$. The only two things that can lower it are a better distance
$\mu$ or a richer probe $h$.

\textbf{Why $L_2$ follows the true error better than cosine.} The true error is an $L_2$
distance $\|\hat O_i-O\|_2$, while cosine looks only at the angle and ignores size,
$d_{\cos}=1-\langle\hat h_i,h\rangle/(\|\hat h_i\|\,\|h\|)$. When predictors differ mainly in
how much they over- or under-shoot, which is what usually goes wrong as the prediction
reaches further ahead, the useful signal is in the size, and cosine misses it, while $L_2$
and $L_1$ keep it. When the map from probe to output is roughly linear near the anchors, the
ordering by size is roughly kept, so the $L_2$ distance is the closer match. This predicts
that the $L_2$ gain should grow with more skipping and more noise, both of which make the
size error larger, and the sweep confirms this (\S\ref{sec:sweep}). We give this as an
explanation, not a theorem; the matching inequality holds in all $21$ settings.

\section{Core results}\label{sec:core}
Table~\ref{tab:core} shows the core study. The $L_2$ distance ranks predictors better than
cosine and picks the best one more often; the gain is real (the confidence interval does not
include zero) but small, a $5\%$ relative gain in Spearman, not the roughly fourfold gain
from the early run. $L_1$ does as well as $L_2$, which confirms that what helps is using
size, not cosine itself. The online-least-squares predictors are worse than fixed Taylor
ones, and adding them to the Taylor set does not help. Picking per token with the probe does
worse than the best fixed predictor here ($+0.004$ in energy distance), yet an oracle that
knows the true errors clearly beats the naive baseline ($-0.005$): the room to improve is
real, but the probe is too weak to use it. So the limit is proxy fidelity, not the predictor
set or the choice rule.

\section{Sensitivity across regimes}\label{sec:sweep}
We vary skipping (cache stride $2$ to $8$), dimension ($2$ to $32$), how far apart the
mixture components are, the noise scale, and the number of components $K$, giving $21$
settings with $5$ seeds each (Fig.~\ref{fig:v2}). \textbf{The $L_2$ gain shows up everywhere
but stays small.} It is positive and significant in all $21$ settings, growing from $+0.012$
at mild settings to $+0.034$ at stride $8$ and $+0.024$ at high noise, which matches the
size-error explanation. \textbf{Online predictors never win.} Taylor is better in $19/21$
settings and online in none; the best single fixed predictor is a Taylor one in every
setting. \textbf{Per-token choice helps only when skipping is aggressive.} Picking per token
with the probe beats the naive best-fixed predictor in just one setting, stride $8$, where
fixed predictors get bad enough that switching per token pays off (probe$-$naive $=-0.074$).
In all milder settings the two curves lie on top of each other (Fig.~\ref{fig:v2}a), so the
gains of ``adaptive'' caching at moderate speedups come from the speedup, not the per-token
choice. The room to improve grows with skipping (Fig.~\ref{fig:v2}c), so the aggressive
settings are exactly where a better probe would help most. \textbf{What matters is proxy
fidelity.} Giving the probe more information (a richer top-$m$ soft readout) makes its
ranking steadily better, from $0.33$ to $0.68$, $0.73$, and $1.0$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:v2}d). A
trained DiT's learned first-layer probe sits much higher on this curve (our companion study
measures Spearman ${\approx}0.77$~\cite{challagundla2025probe}), so our testbed gives a lower
bound and the $L_2$ gain we measure is on the safe side.

\section{Adversarial test suite}
We pre-registered $30$ tests, each meant to break a claim: correctness checks on the testbed
(exact-denoiser limits, $\mathrm{energy}(\text{full},\text{full}){=}0$, and no leakage of the
true error into the choice), the multi-seed core comparisons, the $21$-setting crossovers,
the speed--quality curve going the right way, a budget selector trading compute for quality
in the right direction, and the probe-richness curve. The suite gives \textbf{$26$ passes,
$0$ failures, and $4$ notes}: the $L_2$ gain is small rather than fourfold; the raw proxy
Spearman is only moderate; the end-to-end quality gain of $L_2$ over cosine is tiny; and at
mild skipping the speedup, not the per-token choice, explains the gains. No test broke a
claim that we keep, and the two ideas we dropped failed exactly the tests meant to catch
them.

\section{Discussion}
Most of the results are negative. Of the three ideas, one (online predictors) does not help,
one (per-token choice) helps only when skipping is aggressive, and one (the $L_2$ distance)
gives a small but steady gain because it uses size. They fit together once we see that TAP's
choice is already optimal given the true errors: what is left to improve is proxy fidelity,
set by the distance and, even more, by how rich the probe is. This turns the problem from
tuning the method into measuring the probe, which our exact testbed makes easy.

\section{Limitations}
Our denoiser is a simple Gaussian mixture, not a trained network, and the probe is a shallow
hard readout. These choices let us compute the true answer, but they also make the probe a
weaker ranker than a trained DiT's learned first layer (Fig.~\ref{fig:v2}d
and~\cite{challagundla2025probe}). The energy-distance values depend on the testbed, so all
our conclusions use paired comparisons within the same setting. TAP has no public code, so we
test the method, not a specific implementation. Whether the small $L_2$ gain and the
aggressive-skipping probe benefit grow or shrink on large latent DiTs is still open and needs
more compute than we had.

\section{Conclusion}
We tested three ideas for improving a training-free diffusion predictor and found that most
do not hold. Across many seeds and settings, online predictors do not beat fixed Taylor
ones, per-token choice helps only when skipping is aggressive, and the $L_2$ distance gives a
small but steady gain over cosine because it keeps the size information the true error
depends on. Since per-token choice is already optimal, the only thing that scales is proxy
fidelity, set by the distance and, even more, by how rich the probe is. We suggest testing
these methods on trained networks with fidelity-to-base-model scores, and improving the
probe rather than the choice rule.
